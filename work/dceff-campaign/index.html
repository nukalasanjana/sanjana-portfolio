01 / Overview

The Campaign
Brief & Context

This campaign was developed as part of a hands-on visual storytelling class, where we partnered with the DC Environmental Film Festival (DCEFF) to create a real-world campaign for their annual theme: "Against the Current."

The campaign drew direct inspiration from the documentary "The Bluebird Man" — a film about conservationists dedicated to saving the Eastern Bluebird. Every piece of work was anchored in the film's themes: connecting with nature, advocacy through quiet persistence, and the serenity that comes from doing meaningful environmental work.

The project produced four deliverables: a concept collage and Levi's print design, a perfume product concept ("Nestled"), a DCEFF theme logo, and a full shoot bible for a Levi's × DCEFF fashion campaign — including the final photos and campaign poster.

The Film

"The Bluebird Man" follows a conservationist's lifelong mission to protect Eastern Bluebirds — centering themes of environmental advocacy, patience, and humanity's relationship with nature.

The Brief

Build a visual campaign identity for DCEFF's "Against the Current" theme — spanning product concept, logo design, Levi's print design, and a fashion shoot bible with final photographs.

02 / Concept Collage & Levi's Print Design

From Film to Fabric —
Translating the Bluebird's World

The first assignment was a concept collage capturing the visual essence of "The Bluebird Man." That visual language then became the foundation for a Levi's t-shirt print design.

Levi's T-Shirt Print Design — bluebird concept collage on denim texture

Reading the Film Through a Design Lens

I watched "The Bluebird Man" twice — once for the story, once for the visual language. I noted the recurring palette: soft sky blues, warm rustic browns, the green of meadows, and the orange-rust of the bluebirds' chests. I also noticed the "Long Trail" signage and wooden birdhouse structures that gave the film its rustic throughline.

The mirrored collage composition — sky reflected in water — came from a bathing scene that stuck with me. The concept collage and print both carry that symmetry: nature above, nature below, the bluebird centered in it all.

03 / Product Design

"Nestled" —
A Perfume Born from the Film

Using the visual language of the concept collage, I designed a perfume product inspired by the film's emotional core: the feeling of being nestled in nature, cared for, at home.

Nestled

Scent Notes

Oak and wildflowers with notes of
birdsong echo and morning dew

The bottle is shaped like a birdhouse — referencing the film's focus on creating homes for bluebirds. A ceramic bluebird perches on the wooden cap. The amber-to-clear liquid gradient reflects the film's warm, rustic palette. The name "Nestled" evokes the cozy intimacy of being sheltered in nature.

Generated using Adobe Firefly after multiple rounds of prompting. The biggest challenge: Firefly would lock onto its first interpretation of a prompt and resist refinement — requiring completely reworked prompts rather than incremental adjustments.

"I wanted a name that felt cozy and intimate, not dramatic. 'Nestled' captures the feeling of being held by nature — the bluebirds finding safety in the nesting boxes, and the humans finding peace in caring for them."

04 / Logo Design

"Against the Current"
DCEFF Theme Logo

The second major deliverable was a theme logo for DCEFF's "Against the Current" annual season. I designed two iterations — a bold sans-serif version and an all-script final update.

Version 1 — Bold Resistance

"Against the" in heavy black sans-serif for defiance and clarity. "Current" in liquid blue letterforms — the word itself dissolves, showing the current weakening under change.

Updated Version — Unified Flow

"Against the" shifts to flowing script, creating a unified handwritten quality. The tension becomes subtler — both elements share the same fluid nature, with "Current" still dissolving below.

"I didn't want a literal wave or fish icon — that felt too expected. Instead, I made the word 'Current' itself behave like water: pooling, dripping, barely holding its form. The letterforms dissolve and reform, suggesting that the current — the status quo — weakens under the weight of people who push against it."

Why Liquid Typography?

I went through eight sketch concepts — waves, spirals, mixed scripts, stacked layouts — before landing on the idea of making the word "Current" perform its own meaning. The arrow pointing backwards in the "t" of "Against" quietly reinforces the concept: reversing direction, going against the flow.

Connecting to DCEFF's Mission

DCEFF highlights changemakers — documentary filmmakers, environmental advocates, scientists who push against the current direction of environmental damage. The logo had to feel both defiant and hopeful, just like the films it represents.

06 / Creative Process

How I Got
from Blank to Brief

1

Deep Watching

I watched "The Bluebird Man" twice — once for the story, once for the visual language. I paused on color palettes, noted recurring textures (wood, sky, wildflowers), and tracked the emotional register of each scene. This became my creative brief to myself.

2

Rapid Sketching Without Judgment

The in-class sketching sessions were transformative. I typically get paralyzed trying to make something perfect before it exists. Sketching freely let me generate 8–10 concept directions in a sitting. The birdhouse bottle and the liquid typography both emerged from this low-pressure space.

3

Iterating on Feedback

Each deliverable went through at least one formal revision. Concept collage: fixed the symmetry axis. Nestled: adjusted label typography spacing. Logo: evolved "Against the" from bold sans-serif to script in the final version. The first version is always just the starting point.

4

Working With (and Against) Generative AI

Adobe Firefly was part of the workflow for the perfume bottle imagery. The key learning: Firefly locks onto its initial interpretation and resists incremental adjustments. Instead of adding descriptors, I found it more effective to scrap and restart with an entirely reframed prompt. Working with how the tool thinks became a skill in itself.

5

Building the Shoot Bible as a Communication Document

The shoot bible had to communicate my vision clearly to a photographer, model, and stylist who each had their own expertise. Writing post-production notes — spacing requirements, burst shot instructions, background clearing — taught me that great art direction anticipates editing before you even pick up a camera.

07 / Reflection

What I Took
Away

  • Constraints are generative. Designing for a real client (DCEFF) with a real brief pushed me harder than open-ended prompts ever have.
  • Naming is design. "Nestled" vs. "Bluebird No. 1" — the right name is a creative decision that cascades into every other design choice.
  • Typography can perform meaning. The liquid letterforms in the logo weren't decoration — they were the argument the logo was making.
  • Pre-production thinking saves post-production hours. The shoot bible's precision on framing and spacing was directly tied to compositing feasibility on set.

Biggest Surprise

I expected ideation to be the hard part. Execution — specifically getting AI tools to actualize my vision — was where I spent the most energy and learned the most.

What I'd Do Differently

Start Firefly prompting much earlier. Generative AI rewards iteration volume — I'd give myself more time to experiment with prompt strategies before committing to a direction.

How This Shapes My Practice

This project confirmed I want to work at the intersection of storytelling and visual strategy — using design to communicate ideas that matter, not just to make things look good.

A Campaign Built on
Empathy for a Film

Four deliverables. One through-line: the feeling of standing in nature and believing that small acts of care — for bluebirds, for community, for the environment — add up to something bigger than ourselves. That's what "Against the Current" means to me.
